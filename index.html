Welcome — what this tool does

This builder walks your group through every section of the Final Presentation worksheet in seven guided steps. At each step you'll see a clear explanation, a worked example (Newark Evictions), and a fill-in area for your own group. At the end, it generates a complete presentation outline and a printable summary of all your answers.

Your 7 steps

  1. Topic & Why You Chose It — brainstorm 3 ideas, pick one, explain why.
  2. Systems Question — turn your topic into a "Why does X keep happening even though Y?" question.
  3. BOTG (Behavior Over Time Graph) — pick your variable, then click to sketch the past line plus three futures (Business-as-usual, Feared, Hoped).
  4. Iceberg Analysis — fill in Events → Patterns → Structures → Mental Models.
  5. Connection Circle — enter 4–8 system elements, then tap pairs to add R (reinforcing) and B (balancing) arrows.
  6. High-Leverage Intervention — pick a Meadows level, pick a theorist, and the tool builds your one-sentence argument.
  7. Presentation Outline — auto-generated slide-by-slide outline you can print, screenshot, or copy into PowerPoint.
Heads up: Your work stays in this browser tab only. Use Print → Save as PDF at the end to keep it. If you close this tab without printing, your answers are gone.

Step 1 · Your topic

Brainstorm 3 ideas, pick one, say why.
Worksheet §Part 2-A
The best topics come from genuine curiosity — something your group has noticed, lived near, or wondered about.
Example: We chose evictions in Newark because we have all seen "pay or quit" notices in our neighborhoods and we want to understand why this keeps happening despite new affordable housing being built.

Step 2 · Your systems question

Why does X keep happening even though Y?
Worksheet §Part 2-B
The trick: a systems question asks WHY something keeps happening, not WHAT it is. The "even though" is the most important part — it forces you to explain why the problem hasn't already been fixed.
Example: Evictions in the same Newark zip codes keep spiking every summer, even as the city reports record housing investment.
"Why does [your variable] keep happening in [where] even though [the apparent fix]?"
Example: Why do evictions in Newark keep rising even though the city has invested in affordable housing?
All three should be checked before moving on.

Step 4 · Iceberg Analysis

Events → Patterns → Structures → Mental Models.
Worksheet §Part 3 — Tool 2
The deeper you go, the more leverage you find. Fixing one event helps one family. Changing a structure or belief helps everyone.

👁️Events — what just happened (visible, headline-level)

Example: The Alvarez family on Clinton Ave receives a 3-day pay-or-quit notice in July.

📊Patterns — what keeps happening over time

Example: Eviction filings in Newark spike every summer in the same 5 zip codes.

🏛️Structures — rules & arrangements holding the pattern in place

Example: No local rent control; corporate landlord consolidation (40%+ of multi-family units); filing costs landlords only $56.

🧠Mental Models — beliefs that keep the structures standing

Example: "Housing is a commodity, not a right." "Evictions are tenant failure, not system failure."

Step 5 · Connection Circle

Map your system. Find the loops.
Worksheet §Part 3 — Tool 3
Two kinds of loops you're hunting for. Reinforcing (R) = snowball, drives the problem forward. Balancing (B) = thermostat, pushes back toward stability. You need at least one of each.
Arrow mode: R arrows: 0 B arrows: 0
Click an element to select it (it turns gold). Then click a second element to draw an arrow from first → second. The arrow inherits whichever mode (R or B) is active above.
